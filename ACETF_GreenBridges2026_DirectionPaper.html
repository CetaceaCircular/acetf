01

Purpose of This Paper

This paper is a direction document, not a final conference program. It is intended to prompt committee discussion on the themes, sequencing, and structure of Green Bridges 2026 — and to invite each member to contribute their expertise to the session where they can have most impact.

Energy transition is the subject. Standards interoperability is the mechanism. Australian industry contribution is the opportunity.

Two sectors anchor the program as co-equal pillars:

  • Green Iron (HBI/DRI) — Australia's iron ore and renewable energy endowment makes it the natural production base for hydrogen-based direct reduced iron. The SA–Shandong collaboration is a live proof of concept with Australian Treasury and FIRB already signalling welcome.
  • Sustainable Aviation Fuel (SAF) — South Australia's direct airline connections to China (China Southern, China Eastern, Cathay Pacific) create an immediate, quantifiable CORSIA carbon liability and a concrete feedstock supply opportunity that puts SA at the centre of bilateral SAF collaboration.

The proposed program is organised around three foundations:

  • Standards interoperability as the explicit through-line of the entire forum — not one session among many, but the precondition for capital to move and trade to flow
  • Practical economics throughout — $/tCO2e liabilities, CapEx requirements, and the trade benefit from standards alignment are stated in every session, not just implied
  • Chinese regulatory requirements as the new framing for the AU–China relationship — not about selling, but about collaborating on carbon transparency across the AU–CN–EU triangle

Why Adelaide is the right venue for a SAF forum

China Southern Airlines operates direct Adelaide–Guangzhou services. China Eastern and Cathay Pacific serve Adelaide–China routes. These carriers are CORSIA signatories with real and growing carbon liabilities on exactly these routes. South Australia has the feedstocks — tallow from its livestock industry, waste cooking oils, agricultural residues — and the renewable energy to process them. The SAF certification gap (ISCC vs Chinese scheme vs CORSIA) is the specific standards problem that is preventing South Australian feedstocks from flowing into Chinese airline SAF programs. Green Bridges 2026 is the platform to close it.

The investment unlock from standards alignment

These numbers frame the stakes. But the practical argument for the forum is simpler: Chinese industrial capital will not move upstream to Australian green industries without certification frameworks it recognises. Australian green industries will not achieve EU CBAM exemption without carbon accounting that satisfies EU requirements. And no Australian producer can cost-effectively maintain separate certifications for three markets.

Standards mutual recognition — ACCU + GB/T + ISO 14064 — is not a compliance exercise. It is the architecture that makes AU–CN–EU green trade commercially viable. Every dollar of the A$386 billion green iron opportunity, and every tonne of SAF that could flow from South Australian feedstocks, depends on closing this gap.

03

The Standards–Capital Nexus

Core thesis
China has trillions of renminbi earmarked for industrial decarbonisation — in steel, cement, chemicals, aviation, and shipping. A material share of that capital needs to flow upstream to secure certified green feedstocks: iron ore, hydrogen, SAF, and ammonia. Australia is the natural supplier. But without standards interoperability — without shared or mutually recognised frameworks for carbon accounting, product carbon footprints, and supply chain certification — that money cannot move. The capital is willing. The standards gap is the barrier. This forum exists to close it.

The question in each session is not only 'how does this sector work?' but: what specific standards gap is preventing Chinese industrial decarbonisation capital from flowing to this Australian sector, and what would it take to close it?

China's Ecological & Environmental Code — the Australian intelligence gap

China's Ecological & Environmental Code (effective August 2026) consolidates over 30 environmental laws into a unified framework. For the first time, Dual Carbon goals are binding law — with statutory carbon markets, mandatory carbon footprint management, and green supply chain requirements embedded throughout.

Outside China, awareness of this legislation is close to zero. Australian companies supplying into Chinese industrial value chains — or seeking Chinese investment — will increasingly be required to meet Code standards without knowing what those standards are. ACETF has an opportunity to be the platform that closes this intelligence gap. No other AU–China dialogue mechanism is currently positioned to do this.

Market access
Chinese procurement will require Code compliance
Australian iron ore, hydrogen, and SAF suppliers who cannot demonstrate Code-aligned carbon accounting will lose procurement opportunities to compliant suppliers elsewhere — without ever knowing why.
Investment flow
Chinese FDI is increasingly standards-contingent
Chinese outbound investors need confidence that Australian projects will be certifiable under Chinese frameworks. Where GB/T and ISO diverge, investment decisions stall. Certainty is a prerequisite for capital movement.
Competitive moat
First-mover advantage in mutual recognition
An Australian green iron producer certifiable under both ACCU methodology and GB/T carbon accounting commands a premium and eliminates re-certification costs competitors bear. Getting there first, and getting there together, is the strategic play.
Suggested keynote framing

'The Code You Have Never Heard Of — And Why It Will Determine Whether Australia Wins or Loses the Green Economy.' This positions ACETF as providing intelligence, not just networking.

04

The AU–CN–EU Carbon Transparency Triangle

This forum is not about bilateral trade in the traditional sense. It is about positioning Australian industry at the centre of a trilateral carbon transparency architecture — where Chinese industrial regulatory requirements and EU border carbon mechanisms both converge on the need for certified, traceable, low-carbon products.

🇨🇳
China — the buyer and investor
Chinese industry faces Dual Carbon obligations. Ecological Code mandates supply chain carbon footprint management. Steel, aviation, and chemical sectors need certified low-carbon inputs to meet GB/T standards and ETS compliance requirements.
Needs: GB/T-aligned carbon accounting; PCF certification; green supply chain traceability
🇦🇺
Australia — the supplier
Australia has the iron ore, renewable energy, agricultural feedstocks, and rule-of-law environment. But it currently certifies under ACCU and ISO frameworks. Without mutual recognition, Australian producers carry triple compliance costs to access CN and EU markets simultaneously.
Needs: ACCU + GB/T + ISO mutual recognition; third-party verification accepted in all three markets
🇪🇺
Europe — the CBAM lever
EU CBAM creates a carbon price signal that flows back through global steel supply chains. Chinese steel producers facing CBAM need green inputs certified to EU-acceptable standards. This creates Chinese demand for Australian green iron — if the certification is right.
Needs: ISO 14064 / EU-accepted MRV; CBAM-compliant embedded carbon documentation
The opportunity
An Australian green iron producer certified under ACCU + GB/T + ISO 14064 simultaneously satisfies: Chinese Ecological Code procurement requirements, Chinese ETS compliance inputs, and EU CBAM exemption. This triple certification is the competitive moat that makes Australian green iron commercially dominant — but it requires ACETF to broker the mutual recognition framework between the three systems. The same logic applies to SAF: CORSIA + Chinese certification + ISCC mutual recognition unlocks South Australian feedstocks for every China-route carrier.
